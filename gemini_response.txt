This is a fascinating and highly rigorous dataset. Using a 500 nm thermal oxide substrate for interference enhancement is a textbook-perfect strategy for characterizing ultra-thin, highly absorbing films like pyrolyzed Carbon Molecular Sieves (CMS). Standard silicon substrates would yield severe correlation between thickness and the optical constants for films under 20 nm, but the thick oxide layer breaks this correlation by shifting the phase ($\Delta$) sensitivity.The parameterization of the optical model—incorporating an Effective Medium Approximation (EMA) for surface roughness and a B-Spline for the complex dielectric function—is highly appropriate for capturing the dispersive, non-Kramers-Kronig-constrained intermediate states of pyrolyzing 6FDA-DAM.Here is a comprehensive analysis of the provided data, the structural physics at play, and the implications for membrane science.1. Dielectric Function ComparisonAnalyzing the B-Spline outputs ($n$ and $k$ vs. wavelength) across the samples reveals a clear thickness-dependent divergence in optical properties:Real Part ($n$): In the visible spectrum (e.g., at 632.8 nm), there is a stark increase in the refractive index as the pyrolyzed film becomes thinner. The thickest sample (Si29, ~154 nm final) exhibits an $n \approx 1.986$, whereas the ultra-thin sample (Si16, ~7.7 nm final) spikes to $n \approx 2.075$.Imaginary Part ($k$): The extinction coefficient follows a similar trend, showing increased absorption in the thinner films.Physical Interpretation: In carbonaceous materials, a higher refractive index and extinction coefficient directly correlate with increased electronic conjugation and physical density. The data indicates that the ultra-thin pyrolyzed films possess a more tightly packed, highly aromatized $sp^2$ carbon network compared to their thicker counterparts.2. Thickness & Refractive Index Trends (Volume Collapse)By mapping the pristine states to the pyrolyzed states, we observe a dramatic scale effect in the 1D volume collapse (thickness reduction, as the film is pinned laterally to the substrate):Thick Film Regime (Si29): The film starts at 213.3 nm and pyrolyzes to 153.8 nm. This represents a thickness collapse of roughly 28%.Ultra-Thin Film Regime: While the pristine equivalent for the 7.7 nm Si16 sample isn't perfectly paired in the extracted snippet, extrapolating from the Si12 pristine data (18.1 nm), an 18 nm film collapsing to ~7.7 nm represents a collapse of approximately 57%.The Trend: There is a strong negative correlation between initial thickness and the degree of collapse. Concurrently, the degree of collapse is positively correlated with the final refractive index. Thinner films shrink significantly more and become optically denser.3. Physics of Pyrolysis for 6FDA-DAM at 615 °C6FDA-DAM pyrolyzed at 615 °C is transitioning from a high-free-volume glassy polyimide into a microporous, turbostratic carbon framework. At this specific temperature, the polymer undergoes cleavage of the imide rings, loss of bulky -CF3 groups, and the evolution of CO and CO2, leaving behind cross-linked aromatic strands.The ellipsometry data proves that this carbonization process is not a uniform, bulk-averaged phenomenon. The physical transformation is heavily modulated by the distance from the interfaces (air/polymer and polymer/substrate). The bulk of a 150 nm film retains more "open" carbonaceous structures (higher fractional free volume, lower $n$), while a 10 nm film is forced into a highly collapsed, dense state.4. Hypothesis Generation: Why Thinner Films Collapse MoreTo explain the physics driving this thickness-dependent densification, here are five scientifically grounded hypotheses:Hypothesis 1: Unhindered Volatile Outgassing (The Transport Theory)Pyrolysis involves immense mass loss via the evolution of gases. In a thick film (>150 nm), the diffusion path for these massive degradation byproducts is long. This transient outgassing may act as a blowing agent, creating and temporarily stabilizing micro-voids, resulting in a more open final CMS framework (lower $n$). In sub-20 nm films, volatile desorption is instantaneous, leaving no trapped gases to prop open the collapsing aromatic strands.Hypothesis 2: Substrate-Induced Chain Pre-AlignmentIn the pristine state, polymer chains within the first ~5–10 nm of the SiO2 interface often adopt a flat, 2D conformation due to substrate interactions and spin-coating hydrodynamics. For a 15 nm pristine film, this aligned boundary layer constitutes the entire film. Upon pyrolysis, these pre-aligned chains preferentially form dense, parallel-stacked graphene-like sheets, leading to exceptional out-of-plane collapse and high refractive indices. Thick films are dominated by the isotropic, randomly coiled bulk chains.Hypothesis 3: Enhanced Interfacial Mobility During the Thermal WindowBefore cross-linking solidifies the carbon structure, the polymer passes through a highly mobile state (exceeding its $T_g$). In ultra-thin films, the surface-to-volume ratio is massive. The enhanced mobility of the free surface allows the polymer chains to rearrange and pack much more efficiently into a thermodynamically stable, dense state before the structure rigidifies.Hypothesis 4: Stress Relaxation via 1D CompactionThe film is strongly adhered to the SiO2 substrate, physically preventing in-plane (lateral) shrinkage. All volumetric loss during degradation must be translated into out-of-plane (thickness) collapse. In ultra-thin films, the resulting biaxial tensile stress field spans the entire thickness of the material, forcefully driving the planar aromatic segments to stack tightly against the substrate to minimize strain energy.Hypothesis 5: Altered Degradation Kinetics at the SiO2 BoundaryThe thermal oxide layer may subtly influence the local thermodynamics of the reaction. SiO2 could act as a mild catalytic surface or alter the local thermal conductivity during the precise moment of imide ring cleavage, accelerating the cross-linking and aromatization reactions near the interface compared to the bulk polymer.5. Significance to Membrane ScienceThese findings strike at the heart of gas separation membrane commercialization and scalability.Industrial implementation of CMS membranes relies on asymmetric hollow fibers or thin-film composites, where the active separating layer must be ultra-thin (often sub-100 nm) to achieve commercially viable gas permeance.However, the ellipsometry data demonstrates that extrapolating bulk or thick-film transport properties to the ultra-thin regime is fundamentally flawed. If an engineer optimizes a pyrolysis protocol (like 615 °C) on a 50 µm free-standing film or a 200 nm coated sample, they are optimizing a relatively open, low-density carbon structure.When that exact same protocol is applied to a 20 nm skin layer on a composite membrane, the material will undergo extreme volume collapse. The resulting CMS skin will have drastically reduced free volume, narrowed micropores, and a significantly higher refractive index. In terms of separation performance, this ultra-thin skin will likely exhibit much lower permeance and much higher selectivity than the thick-film data would predict. To successfully scale CMS membranes, the pyrolysis protocols must be explicitly mapped and tuned as a function of the precursor's geometric confinement, utilizing precise optical metrology to track the true state of the active layer.
